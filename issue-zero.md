# Issue Zero: The First Creditor Goes Home

*Published June 9, 2026*

*America's biggest lender just got a reason to stop lending to America. The proof arrives on a schedule, not a guess. This memo tells you the date and the number to watch.*

---

## The short version

For forty years, the safest assumption in the financial world has been this: when America borrows, somebody always shows up to lend.

The biggest of those somebodies is Japan. Japan holds about $1.2 trillion in US Treasuries. Treasuries are loans to the US government. You hand over money, and the government pays you interest. Japan is America's largest foreign lender.

Here is what just changed. For the first time in a generation, Japan earns more interest lending to its own government than lending to ours. Japan's 20-year bonds now pay more than America's 20-year bonds.

So ask the obvious question. If you got paid more to lend at home, with no currency risk, why would you keep lending abroad?

That question now sits under the entire US bond market. And under the "safe part" of a lot of retirement accounts.

## Why you should care

Bond prices work like anything else. They need buyers. When the biggest buyer steps back, prices fall and interest rates rise. That moves mortgages, stock prices, and the dollar itself.

You have already felt a small preview. 2022 was the worst year for bonds in modern history. The "safe part" of the standard retirement portfolio failed exactly when it was supposed to protect you.

That is what it feels like when buyers thin out. Now imagine the largest foreign buyer on the planet deciding home pays better.

## What the headlines got wrong

This spring, headlines said it was already happening. March 2026 showed the biggest monthly wave of Japanese money coming home ever recorded.

But there is a catch the headlines skipped. Japan's business year ends March 31. Japanese money always floods home in March, every single year, for accounting reasons. Judging this story by March is like judging a toy store by its December sales.

So the record March number proves nothing yet. It might be the start of something historic. It might be the same seasonal wave as always.

Here is the good news: nobody has to guess.

## The answer prints on a schedule

Every month, the US Treasury publishes a scoreboard called the TIC report. It shows exactly which foreign countries bought or sold American bonds.

Around mid-June, the report covering April comes out. Around mid-July, the one covering May. April and May are normal months, with no accounting deadline to muddy the water.

Two months in a row of Japanese selling would mean the turn is real. Continued buying would mean the March wave was seasonal, and the scary headlines were early.

Simple as that. One number, two dates, and the biggest question in the bond market gets its answer.

## What we are putting on the record

This desk publishes its claims before the outcome, with the test that proves each one wrong. Here they are.

**1. The March record proves nothing by itself.** It is probably the usual year-end wave. We find out when the April report prints in mid-June. If April and May both show Japanese selling, the turn is real, and our caution was wrong.

**2. There is no smart trade on this story today.** Nothing forces the issue on any date. The big Japanese institutions are not positioning for it. So our verdict right now is: do nothing. Stand down.

**3. The real fireworks have a trigger number: 140 yen.** Today one US dollar buys about 160 yen. Japan's giant life insurance companies, some of the biggest investors alive, are betting the dollar stays strong. They carry less protection against a falling dollar than they have in decades. If the dollar drops below 140 yen and stays there, those insurers are forced to reverse, all at once, buying yen into a rising market. That is when this story becomes an event. Until 140 breaks, it is a story.

**4. Watch Japan's long-term bond auctions.** When Japan's government struggles to sell its longest bonds, the stress spreads to long-term rates everywhere, including ours. These auctions are scheduled and public. Each weak one is a warning shot.

**5. Wall Street says today's rising long-term rates are temporary, caused by oil.** If we are right, the deeper cause is the missing buyer. And rates will not calm down when oil does. We find out by late this year.

## The trade we already turned down

You should know what this desk refused, and why. That is part of the record too.

In early June, with the "Japanese money coming home" story all over the financial press, we built the obvious trade: bet on a rising yen. Then we checked it against the spending plans Japan's life insurers had just published in late April.

Those plans showed the opposite of the headline. The insurers are the least protected against a rising yen they have been in decades, and they plan to stay that way. The biggest money in Japan is not acting like the story is real yet. No scheduled event forces the issue. So we passed.

The dollar sat near 159.66 yen the day we passed, and near 159.90 the day after. So far the market agrees with us. But this call is not graded yet. The June and July reports grade it. If they prove a real turn is underway right now, our pass was wrong, and this page will say so in plain words.

## What to do now

**With your money: nothing.** That is the honest answer, and you will notice nobody selling something ever gives it.

**With your calendar: mark mid-June.** The April TIC report is the first real test. We will read it and tell you, in one sentence, seasonal or structural.

**With your memory: keep one number, 140.** If you ever see the dollar fall below 140 yen and stay there, you will know something most people watching headlines do not: the forced buying has begun.

You now know which number actually decides this story, and the dates it gets decided. That puts you ahead of almost everyone reading the same headlines.
